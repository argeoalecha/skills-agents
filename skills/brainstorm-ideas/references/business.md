# Business Problem Methodologies

Use for strategy, GTM, pricing, product, and market problems.

---

## 1. Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD)
**Source:** Clayton Christensen, Harvard Business School — *The Innovator's Dilemma* (1997); formalized with Tony Ulwick in *Competing Against Luck* (Christensen, 2016); further developed by Strategyn (Tony Ulwick) and Bob Moesta
**Answers:** What outcome is the customer actually trying to achieve — and what are they "hiring" a solution to do?
**Best for:** Product-market fit, feature prioritization, positioning, pricing rationale

### Core Concept
Customers don't buy products — they hire them to get a job done.
Every job has three dimensions:
- **Functional:** The practical task (e.g., "get financial reports to my accountant")
- **Emotional:** How they want to feel (e.g., "feel in control of my business")
- **Social:** How they want to be perceived (e.g., "look like a serious business owner")

### Application
1. Define the target customer segment (be specific — a role in a context, not a demographic)
2. Identify the **main job** they're trying to get done in the situation where they'd use your solution
3. Map the **job steps** (what they do before, during, and after the job)
4. Identify **underserved outcomes** — steps that are painful, slow, uncertain, or expensive
5. Design the solution to nail those underserved outcomes
6. Use the job statement in positioning: "When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [outcome]"

### Job Statement Template
> "When [situation/trigger], I want to [action/motivation], so I can [desired outcome]."

### Example — Hayah-AI client (Philippine SMB owner)
- **Situation:** End of month, preparing for accountant and bank review
- **Main job:** Understand where money went and whether the business is healthy
- **Underserved outcomes:** Takes too long, numbers don't make sense, no one to explain them
- **Solution angle:** Not a dashboard — a "monthly financial interpreter" that produces a plain-language summary with 3 key decisions to make this month

## 2. Value Proposition Canvas
**Source:** Alexander Osterwalder, Yves Pigneur, Greg Bernarda, Alan Smith — *Value Proposition Design* (Strategyzer, 2014)
**Answers:** Does our solution actually address what customers care about — or are we guessing?
**Best for:** Validating product-market fit, refining messaging, identifying gaps before building

### The Two Sides

**Customer Profile (right side):**
- **Jobs:** What the customer is trying to accomplish (functional, social, emotional)
- **Pains:** Frustrations, risks, obstacles, and bad outcomes they want to avoid
- **Gains:** Benefits, outcomes, and aspirations they want to achieve

**Value Map (left side):**
- **Products & Services:** What you offer
- **Pain Relievers:** How your offering reduces specific pains
- **Gain Creators:** How your offering produces specific gains

### Application
1. Fill the Customer Profile first — based on real customer conversations, not assumptions
2. Fill the Value Map based on what your product/service actually does
3. Identify **fit**: Do your Pain Relievers address the top-ranked Pains? Do your Gain Creators address the top-ranked Gains?
4. Gaps between Value Map and Customer Profile = product or messaging problems
5. Use this to generate solution ideas that close the gaps

### Fit Test
Strong fit = your solution addresses the **most important** jobs, pains, and gains — not all of them, just the critical ones.

## 3. Porter's Five Forces
**Source:** Michael E. Porter, Harvard Business School — *Competitive Strategy* (1979), Harvard Business Review
**Answers:** What structural forces shape competition and profitability in this market?
**Best for:** Market entry decisions, pricing strategy, competitive positioning, partnership vs. build decisions

### The Five Forces

| Force | Key Question | High Threat Signals |
|---|---|---|
| **Threat of new entrants** | How easy is it for new competitors to enter? | Low capital requirements, no switching costs, no regulatory barriers |
| **Bargaining power of suppliers** | How much leverage do your input providers have? | Few alternatives, critical inputs, high switching cost |
| **Bargaining power of buyers** | How much leverage do your customers have? | Price-sensitive, many alternatives, low switching cost, large volume buyers |
| **Threat of substitutes** | Can customers get the same job done another way? | Better price-performance ratio alternatives, low switching cost |
| **Competitive rivalry** | How intense is competition among existing players? | Many similar competitors, low differentiation, slow growth market |

### Application
1. Define the industry/market boundary clearly (too broad = useless analysis)
2. Rate each force: Low / Medium / High threat
3. Identify which forces most constrain profitability
4. Generate strategic options that reduce the impact of the strongest forces
5. Identify white space where forces are weak — those are the best entry points

### Example — AI consulting in Philippine SMB market
- **New entrants:** High threat — low capital, many freelancers entering AI space
- **Buyer power:** High — SMBs are price-sensitive, many options, low switching cost
- **Substitutes:** Medium — DIY tools (ChatGPT, Notion AI) are imperfect substitutes
- **Rivalry:** Medium — few specialized AI consultants, but general IT firms are adjacent
- **Supplier power:** Low — LLM APIs are commoditizing

**Strategic implication:** Differentiate on domain expertise (not AI capability) and raise switching cost through proprietary systems and embedded workflows.

## 4. Double Diamond
**Source:** UK Design Council, 2005 — formalized as a design process framework; widely adopted in product development, service design, and innovation programs globally
**Answers:** Are we solving the right problem before we solve the problem right?
**Best for:** Product development, service design, innovation projects — any situation where the problem itself may be poorly defined

### The Four Phases

```
        Diverge          Converge       Diverge          Converge
         ◇◇◇◇             ◆◆◆             ◇◇◇◇             ◆◆◆
    ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    DISCOVER          DEFINE          DEVELOP          DELIVER
    (explore the      (define the     (explore the     (ship the
     problem space)    right problem)  solution space)   right solution)
```

**Diamond 1 — Problem Space:**
- **Discover:** Research broadly — user interviews, observations, data, analogous markets
- **Define:** Synthesize findings into a clear, validated problem statement (the HMW statement)

**Diamond 2 — Solution Space:**
- **Develop:** Ideate broadly — generate many possible solutions without judgment
- **Deliver:** Test, iterate, and ship the solution that best fits the defined problem

### Application
1. Identify which diamond you're currently in — are you still defining the problem or already building solutions?
2. If you're in Diamond 2 but the problem isn't clearly defined, go back to Diamond 1 first
3. Use MECE + 5 Whys + HMW to complete Diamond 1
4. Use SCAMPER, Six Hats, JTBD to fuel Diamond 2's Develop phase
5. Use convergence methods (ICE, RICE) to complete Diamond 2's Deliver phase

### Common Failure Mode
Teams skip Diamond 1 entirely — they jump straight to building solutions for a problem that hasn't been validated. The Double Diamond is a forcing function to slow down before the expensive phase.
