# Ideation Methodologies

Use these in the divergent phase to generate a wide, diverse set of ideas.
Select 1–2 based on problem type. Do not apply all at once.

---

## 1. SCAMPER
**Source:** Bob Eberle, *SCAMPER: Games for Imagination Development* (1971), building on Alex Osborn's creative thinking checklists (1953)
**Answers:** How can we systematically mutate or recombine existing ideas, products, or processes?
**Best for:** Improving or innovating on something that already exists

### The Seven Lenses

| Letter | Action | Key Question |
|---|---|---|
| **S** | Substitute | What component, material, process, or person can be replaced? |
| **C** | Combine | What ideas, features, or functions can be merged? |
| **A** | Adapt | What else is similar? What can be borrowed from another domain? |
| **M** | Modify / Magnify / Minimize | What can be scaled up, scaled down, or changed in form? |
| **P** | Put to other use | Who else could use this? What other problems does it solve? |
| **E** | Eliminate | What can be removed, simplified, or stripped out entirely? |
| **R** | Reverse / Rearrange | What if the order was inverted? What if the roles were swapped? |

### Application
1. Define the subject (product, process, or service to improve)
2. Apply each lens in order — generate at least 1–2 ideas per lens
3. Keep all ideas without judgment
4. Move to convergence to filter

### Example — AI consulting onboarding process
- **Substitute:** Replace kickoff call with an async AI-guided intake form
- **Combine:** Merge discovery and scoping into a single AI-facilitated workshop
- **Adapt:** Borrow the "product demo before contract" model from SaaS sales
- **Eliminate:** Remove weekly status calls; replace with auto-generated dashboards

## 2. Six Thinking Hats
**Source:** Edward de Bono, *Six Thinking Hats* (1985), Little, Brown and Company
**Answers:** What does this problem look like from six fundamentally different cognitive perspectives?
**Best for:** Group or solo decisions where bias or groupthink is a risk; evaluating ideas under different lenses simultaneously

### The Six Hats

| Hat | Mode | Focus |
|---|---|---|
| White | Facts & Data | What do we know? What data is missing? What are the objective facts? |
| Yellow | Optimism | What are the best-case outcomes? What is the value if this works? |
| Black | Caution | What can go wrong? What are the risks, flaws, and obstacles? |
| Green | Creativity | What are the new ideas? What alternatives exist? What if we broke the rules? |
| Red | Emotion & Intuition | What is the gut reaction? What do stakeholders feel, even if irrational? |
| Blue | Process | What is the thinking process itself? What should we do next? |

### Application
1. Define the problem or idea to evaluate
2. Apply each hat in sequence (suggested order: Blue → White → Green → Yellow → Black → Red → Blue)
3. Spend 2–5 minutes per hat — stay in that mode, do not mix perspectives
4. Blue hat opens and closes the session — it governs the process
5. Synthesize findings into actionable insights

### Example — Should Hayah-AI offer a fixed-price retainer or hourly billing?
- **White:** Average Philippine SMB budget for tech consulting is ₱15k–₱50k/month. Market data on retainer adoption is thin locally.
- **Yellow:** Retainers provide predictable MRR, deepen client relationships, reduce sales overhead.
- **Black:** Scope creep risk is high with retainers; clients may underuse and churn; harder to price first engagement.
- **Green:** Hybrid model — starter project at fixed price, automatic retainer conversion after delivery milestone.
- **Red:** Clients may distrust retainers if they don't see immediate output — need visible monthly deliverables.
- **Blue:** Decision requires more data on client budget patterns before committing; run a 2-client pilot first.

## 3. TRIZ Inventive Principles
**Source:** Genrich Altshuller, developed from 1946 onward through analysis of 400,000+ patents; formalized in *Creativity as an Exact Science* (1984)
**Answers:** How do we resolve a contradiction between two competing requirements without compromise?
**Best for:** Technical problems and engineering trade-offs; business problems with clear opposing constraints

### Core Concept
Most hard problems contain a **contradiction** — improving one thing degrades another.
TRIZ provides 40 inventive principles for resolving these without accepting the trade-off.

### Common Business/Technical Contradictions and Applicable Principles

| Contradiction | TRIZ Principle | Description |
|---|---|---|
| Fast vs. Accurate | **Preliminary action** (#10) | Pre-process or pre-stage work before it's needed |
| Flexible vs. Secure | **Segmentation** (#1) | Divide into parts with different access/flexibility levels |
| Low cost vs. High quality | **Copying** (#26) | Replace expensive original with inexpensive simulation |
| Simple vs. Feature-rich | **Dynamics** (#15) | Make the system adaptable rather than fixed |
| Scalable vs. Personal | **Self-service** (#25) | Let the object/user serve themselves |
| Fast delivery vs. Deep customization | **Preliminary anti-action** (#9) | Counteract known problems before they occur |

### Application
1. Define the system or process to improve
2. Identify the primary contradiction: "If I improve X, then Y gets worse"
3. Look up the contradiction pair in the TRIZ matrix or use the principles above as a guide
4. Apply the matching principle(s) to generate non-compromise solutions

### Example
Contradiction: Hayah-AI proposals need to be detailed (builds trust) but fast to produce (wins more bids).
- **Principle #10 — Preliminary action:** Build a modular proposal library pre-written by domain; assemble per client rather than writing from scratch.
- **Principle #26 — Copying:** Use AI to generate the first draft; human refines only the unique sections.

## 4. Lateral Thinking
**Source:** Edward de Bono, *Lateral Thinking: Creativity Step by Step* (1967), Harper & Row
**Answers:** What solution exists outside the dominant logic pattern we've been assuming?
**Best for:** Problems where every conventional approach has been tried; breaking out of entrenched thinking

### Core Techniques

**Provocation (PO):**
Introduce a deliberately absurd statement, then use it as a springboard.
Structure: "PO [impossible/absurd statement]" → "What would have to be true for this to work?" → extract practical ideas

**Random Entry:**
Pick a completely unrelated word or concept. Force connections between it and the problem.
Useful for unlocking non-obvious analogies.

**Challenge:**
Ask "Why do we do it this way?" about every assumption in the current approach.
Not looking for reasons — looking for whether the assumption is necessary at all.

**Reversal:**
Instead of solving the problem, define how you would make it worse.
Then invert every item on that list.

### Application
1. Pick one technique based on how stuck you are
2. Generate provocations or reversals without censorship
3. Extract practical ideas from even the most absurd provocations
4. Do not evaluate during generation — defer judgment

### Example — "How do we get more referrals from existing Hayah-AI clients?"
**Reversal technique:**
How would we *guarantee* clients never refer us?
- Give them nothing to share
- Make our work feel ordinary
- Never ask
- Deliver late
- Keep them dependent, not empowered

**Inversion:**
- Give clients a shareable artifact after every engagement (insight report, one-pager)
- Make the work feel transformational — not just functional
- Ask explicitly at the close of every project
- Deliver early, with a summary of what changed
- Build client capability, not dependency — they'll talk about that
